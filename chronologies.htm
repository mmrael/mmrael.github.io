INTRO MEDIEVAL 19th CENTURY 1960s-1980s 2025

What do we think of when we think of 'Medieval' design?
Stained glass? The blood of knights spilled under lapis skies? Twinkling stars? The robes of kings alongside the metal of armour?

What about dragons? Holy grails? Caverns concealing mountains of gold?

The Consumer Aesthetics Research Institute identifies a design trend they call ‘Medieval Revival,’ dated from the mid-1950s through the mid-1970s.

But one doesn’t have to peruse the Institute’s archive to recall the boom in pop culture.
⤇⤇
The epics of mid-century Hollywood gave us historical dramas A Man for All Seasons (1966) and The Lion in Winter (1968), which then gave way to medieval fantasy films such Camelot (1967), Donkey Skin (1970), Monty Python and the Holy Grail (1975), the Rankin/Bass The Hobbit (1977), the Ralph Bakshi-directed The Lord of the Rings (1978), and Excalibur (1981).

Dungeons & Dragons emerged in 1974. Artists like Frank Frazetta, The Brothers Hildebrandt, and Roger Dean used Medieval and chivalric motifs in their promotional artwork.
⤇⤇
The resurgence wasn’t limited to visual media. Led Zeppelin included explicit Tolkien references in its fourth studio album (“Misty Mountain Top,” “Battle of Evermore,” “Stairway to Heaven.”). Yes, Jethro Tull, Black Sabbath used Medieval and fantasy motifs in lyrics, stage design, and album art.

California’s Renaissance Pleasure Faire debuted in 1963. Writer and scholar of medieval studies Diana Paxson founded The Society for Creative Anachronism – blending live-action roleplay, medieval reenactment, and scholarly research – in 1966.
⤇⤇
Medievalism had us in a chokehold. But it all feels a bit fanciful, doesn’t it?

Writer Charlotte Amy Landrum observes that the film Perceval (1978) “fully lean[s] into the artifice that Mid-Century Medieval evoked: aesthetics over historical accuracy and the uncanniness … of ancient architecture and clothing styles being replicated in the modern day [...] Perceval rides his horse around sculpted metal trees and enters castles made of cardboard.” She compares the set design to that of a Medieval Times restaurant – “a cheap rendition of an era when people wore fine jewels and lived in towers.”
⤇⤇
What draws us to this artifice? Why do we call it Medieval? How much of it, if any, is based in historical reverence?

Don your armour and mount your house - we're embarking on a quest to track the evolution of what we call 'Medievalism.'
